Petrology and mineralogy are the twin pillars of geological science. From the basaltic ocean floor to the granite cores of mountain ranges, every rock tells a story of pressure, heat, time, and transformation.

This reference compiles the core knowledge that geology students need — classification systems, formation processes, and the grand timeline of Earth's lithic evolution.

Rock Types & Formation

🌋

Igneous Rock

Formed from the solidification of molten magma. Can be intrusive (plutonic) or extrusive (volcanic) depending on where cooling occurs. Granite, basalt, and obsidian are classic examples.

Igneous Mohs 5–7
🏜️

Sedimentary Rock

Composed of particles derived from pre-existing rocks or biological material. Often contains fossils and forms in horizontal layers called strata. Limestone, sandstone, and shale are common forms.

Sedimentary Mohs 2–5
💎

Metamorphic Rock

Rocks transformed by intense heat and pressure deep within the earth. The original mineralogy is altered without melting. Marble forms from limestone; slate from mudstone or shale.

Metamorphic Mohs 3–8
🔮

Minerals & Crystals

Naturally occurring inorganic substances with a defined chemical composition and ordered crystal structure. The Mohs scale from talc (1) to diamond (10) measures their relative hardness.

Mineralogy Mohs 1–10
⛰️

Volcanic Deposits

Pumice, obsidian, basalt, and tephra from explosive or effusive eruptions. Formed at tectonic subduction zones and hotspots where magma breaches the surface.

Volcanic Variable
🌊

Coastal Formations

Sea stacks, arches, wave-cut platforms, and sea caves sculpted by ocean waves, tidal action, and chemical weathering along shorelines over thousands of years.

Erosional Mohs 2–4

The Rock Cycle

Stage 01
01

Weathering & Erosion

Surface rocks are broken down by physical forces (freeze-thaw, abrasion) and chemical processes (oxidation, hydration). Sediment is transported by rivers, wind, and glaciers to depositional basins.

Stage 02
02

Compaction & Lithification

Accumulated sediment is compressed under its own weight and cemented by minerals precipitated from pore water. Over millions of years loose particles become solid sedimentary rock strata.

Stage 03
03

Subduction & Melting

Tectonic plate collision drives rock deep into the mantle. Extreme heat and pressure either metamorphoses the rock or melts it entirely into magma, which may return to the surface via volcanic activity.

Geological Time Scale

4,540 Mya

Hadean Eon — Formation of Earth

Earth accretes from the solar nebula. Intense bombardment by meteorites; a Mars-sized body collides with Earth, ejecting material that forms the Moon. The surface is largely molten.

4,000 Mya

Archean Eon — First Crust

Earth cools sufficiently to form a solid crust. The oldest known rocks — the Acasta Gneiss of Canada — date to this period. Single-celled life emerges in primordial oceans.

541 Mya

Cambrian Explosion

A rapid diversification of complex multicellular organisms leaves an abundant fossil record in sedimentary rock. Hard shells and exoskeletons preserve evidence of ancient life for geologists today.

250 Mya

Permian–Triassic Extinction

The largest mass extinction in Earth's history eliminates ~96% of marine species. Vast volcanic eruptions in Siberia release CO₂ and SO₂, leaving a geochemical signature visible in the stratigraphic record.

66 Mya

K–Pg Boundary

An asteroid impact deposits a global iridium-rich layer visible in outcrops worldwide. The end of the Cretaceous is marked by this thin dark band in the geological record — a precise timestamp in stone.

Rock Cycle · Petrology

Understanding the Rock Cycle: How Rocks Transform Over Geological Time

The rock cycle is one of geology's most elegant concepts — a continuous system by which rocks are created, altered, destroyed, and reformed.

The rock cycle is one of geology's most elegant concepts — a continuous, dynamic system by which rocks are created, altered, destroyed, and reformed through the interplay of internal earth processes and surface weathering. No rock type is permanent; each exists only as a temporary arrangement of matter within an ongoing planetary cycle.

The Three Pathways

Granite, a familiar igneous rock, may be uplifted by tectonic forces, exposed at the surface, and broken down over millennia by rain, frost, and wind. The resulting sediment is carried by rivers to ocean basins, where it accumulates in layers, compacts under its own weight, and slowly lithifies into sandstone or shale. Should tectonic forces drive that sedimentary rock back into the crust, intense heat and pressure transform it into metamorphic schist or gneiss. If temperatures rise high enough, it melts entirely — rejoining the magma reservoir from which new igneous rock will eventually form.

"Every rock is a record of a journey through the earth's interior — a letter written in mineral and time."

Radiometric Dating: Reading the Clock in Stone

Geologists use radioactive isotope dating to trace these transformations across billions of years. When magma crystallises, certain minerals incorporate radioactive parent isotopes (such as uranium-238 or potassium-40) into their lattice structure. These decay at known, constant rates into stable daughter isotopes. By measuring the ratio of parent to daughter in a sample, geologists can calculate the precise time elapsed since the rock formed.

Uranium-lead dating is suited for very ancient rocks — it can reliably date samples billions of years old, including the oldest known terrestrial zircons from Western Australia, which date to 4.4 billion years. Potassium-argon dating is used for volcanic rocks as young as 100,000 years, while rubidium-strontium systems unlock evidence from the deepest Precambrian. Together these systems provide an overlapping chronological framework that has allowed geologists to build the geological time scale with remarkable precision.

Key Fact

The oldest mineral grains ever discovered — Jack Hills zircons from Western Australia — are approximately 4.404 billion years old, just 150 million years younger than Earth itself.

Why the Cycle Matters

Understanding the rock cycle is foundational not just to geology but to climate science, resource extraction, and even planetary science. The cycling of carbon between rocks, ocean, and atmosphere over millions of years regulates Earth's long-term climate. The concentration of metallic ores in certain rock types and geological settings — a product of magmatic differentiation and hydrothermal activity — determines where valuable mineral deposits are found. And by comparing Earth's rock record with surface features on Mars, the Moon, and other bodies, planetary geologists can infer which of these worlds have active or extinct rock cycles of their own.

Mineralogy · Crystal Structure

The Mohs Scale and the Physics of Mineral Hardness

Friedrich Mohs' 1812 ranking of ten minerals by scratch resistance remains one of the most practically useful tools in geological fieldwork — but what does hardness actually measure?

Friedrich Mohs was a German mineralogist working in Vienna in the early nineteenth century when he published his famous hardness scale — a deceptively simple ranking of ten reference minerals from softest to hardest. Two centuries later, his scale remains a standard tool in geological fieldwork, though its underlying physics are more nuanced than the ordinal numbers suggest.

What the Scale Actually Measures

Hardness in the Mohs sense refers specifically to scratch resistance — the ability of a mineral's surface to resist being abraded by another material. A mineral rated higher on the scale will scratch one rated lower; two minerals of equal hardness will leave no mark on each other. Mohs' ten reference points, from talc (1) through gypsum, calcite, fluorite, apatite, feldspar, quartz, topaz, and corundum, to diamond (10), provide a practical field comparison set.

Importantly, the Mohs scale is ordinal, not linear. The physical difference between corundum (9) and diamond (10) is vastly greater than between, say, calcite (3) and fluorite (4). Diamond is roughly 1,500 times harder than corundum by absolute hardness measures such as the Vickers or Knoop indentation tests used in materials science. Mohs' scale compresses this enormous range into a convenient 1–10 format intended for field use, not precision engineering.

"Diamond does not merely scratch corundum — it is in a class so far removed that the gap dwarfs all others on the scale combined."

The Crystal Chemistry Behind Hardness

Hardness is ultimately an expression of bond strength within a mineral's crystal lattice. Diamond — pure carbon arranged in a tetrahedral covalent lattice — achieves its extraordinary hardness because every carbon atom forms four strong covalent bonds in all directions, creating an isotropic three-dimensional network of exceptional strength. Graphite, also pure carbon but arranged in hexagonal sheets bonded only weakly between layers, registers just 1–2 on the Mohs scale.

This illustrates a key principle: chemical composition alone does not determine hardness — crystal structure is equally critical. Kyanite offers a striking example of anisotropic hardness: it measures approximately 5.5 along its length but 7 across it, due to the directional nature of bonding in its aluminosilicate crystal structure. Any mineral identification that relies on hardness must account for the direction of the scratch test.

Field Tip

A fingernail (~2.5), a copper coin (~3.5), a steel knife blade (~5.5), and a glass plate (~5.5) make practical field substitutes for Mohs reference minerals. A quartz crystal carried as a field tool will scratch anything below 7.

Beyond Mohs: Modern Hardness Measurement

For industrial applications — abrasives, cutting tools, wear-resistant coatings — the Mohs scale lacks the precision required. The Vickers hardness test, which measures the indentation left by a pyramid-shaped diamond indenter under a known load, gives quantitative values in megapascals. Synthetic materials such as cubic boron nitride and wurtzite boron nitride have now been engineered to approach or even briefly exceed diamond in hardness under certain loading conditions, pushing the frontier of materials science into territory Mohs could not have anticipated.

Plate Tectonics · Geodynamics

Plate Tectonics: The Engine Driving Earth's Geological Activity

The theory of plate tectonics, consolidated in the 1960s, unified decades of puzzling observations — continental shapes, fossil distributions, volcanic patterns — into a single coherent framework.

The theory of plate tectonics stands as one of the great unifying frameworks of Earth science. Consolidated from the work of Alfred Wegener, Harry Hess, and dozens of other geoscientists through the mid-twentieth century, it explains the distribution of earthquakes and volcanoes, the shapes of ocean basins, the building of mountain ranges, and the long-term cycling of elements between the crust, mantle, oceans, and atmosphere.

The Lithospheric Plates

Earth's outermost rigid layer — the lithosphere, comprising the crust and the uppermost mantle — is broken into roughly fifteen major plates and several smaller ones. These plates, between 100 and 250 kilometres thick, ride atop the asthenosphere: a zone of the upper mantle that is hot enough to flow plastically over geological timescales, despite being solid rock. The driving forces for plate motion include convection currents in the mantle, the gravitational pull of dense, cold oceanic crust descending at subduction zones (slab pull), and the push of newly formed crust at mid-ocean ridges (ridge push).

"The continents do not drift through the ocean floor — they are carried upon it, passengers on vast slabs of lithosphere in slow but relentless motion."

Three Types of Plate Boundary

Where plates meet, three distinct boundary types produce markedly different geological outcomes. At divergent boundaries, such as the Mid-Atlantic Ridge, plates move apart and new oceanic crust forms continuously from upwelling basaltic magma — a process called seafloor spreading. Iceland sits atop this boundary and is being literally pulled apart at a rate of roughly 2.5 centimetres per year.

At convergent boundaries, one plate descends beneath another in the process of subduction. Where oceanic crust meets continental crust, the denser oceanic plate invariably subducts, generating chains of volcanoes (like the Cascades or the Andes), deep oceanic trenches, and powerful earthquakes. Where two continental plates collide — as India has been doing with Asia for the past 50 million years — neither subducts easily, and the result is the crumpling and thickening of crust that built the Himalayas, the highest mountain range on Earth.

At transform boundaries, plates slide horizontally past each other without creating or destroying crust. The San Andreas Fault in California is the most studied example — a right-lateral strike-slip fault along which the Pacific Plate moves northwest relative to the North American Plate at about 46 millimetres per year, accumulating elastic strain that periodically releases in major earthquakes.

Scale of Motion

Tectonic plates move at roughly the same rate fingernails grow — between 1 and 10 centimetres per year. Over 200 million years, this motion has moved continents thousands of kilometres from their original positions.

The Wilson Cycle and Supercontinents

On timescales of hundreds of millions of years, the opening and closing of ocean basins follows a pattern known as the Wilson Cycle. Oceans open at rifts, expand via seafloor spreading, then close as subduction consumes their floor, eventually colliding the flanking continents together into a supercontinent. Pangaea — the last such supercontinent — assembled roughly 300 million years ago and began breaking apart around 180 million years ago, producing the Atlantic Ocean and the geography we recognise today. The geological evidence suggests at least two earlier supercontinents, Rodinia and Nuna, in Earth's deep past, and models predict a future supercontinent — sometimes called Pangaea Proxima — will assemble in approximately 250 million years.

Stratigraphy · Deep Time

Reading the Stratigraphic Record: Earth's History in Layers

Stratigraphy — the study of rock strata — allows geologists to reconstruct billions of years of Earth history from the sequence, composition, and fossils of layered rocks.

If the rock cycle describes how rocks transform, stratigraphy describes how they accumulate in sequence — and in that sequence lies a record of Earth's history that geologists have been decoding for over two centuries. The principle is deceptively simple: in any undisturbed series of sedimentary layers, the oldest beds lie at the bottom and the youngest at the top. This law of superposition, formalised by Nicolas Steno in the seventeenth century, is the cornerstone of historical geology.

Relative and Absolute Time

Stratigraphy originally produced only relative timescales — rock unit A is older than unit B, which predates unit C — without assigning any numerical ages. The development of biostratigraphy in the nineteenth century improved resolution enormously: certain fossil species (index fossils) have known, restricted time ranges, and their presence in a rock layer constrains when that layer was deposited. The global stratigraphic column — the idealised sequence of all rock units from oldest to youngest — was largely assembled from relative methods before radiometric dating provided the absolute age calibration that underpins the modern geological time scale.

"The strata of the earth are a history written in stone — but it is a history with many pages torn out, chapters missing, and margins annotated by time."

Unconformities: The Missing Pages

One of stratigraphy's most important concepts is the unconformity — a surface that represents a gap in the geological record, where erosion or non-deposition has removed or prevented the accumulation of sediment for a period of time. When James Hutton examined the angular unconformity at Siccar Point in Scotland in 1788, he recognised that the tilted rocks below the contact had been deposited, lithified, tilted by mountain building, eroded flat, then buried under new horizontal sediments above — a sequence requiring vast amounts of time. His insight led to the foundational geological concept of deep time: the Earth must be incomprehensibly old for such processes to operate.

Angular unconformities (where beds below and above meet at an angle), disconformities (where both are parallel but a gap exists), and nonconformities (where sedimentary rocks overlie igneous or metamorphic basement) each provide different evidence of the tectonic and depositional history of a region.

The Global Boundary Stratotype

Major divisions of the geological time scale are formally defined by Global Boundary Stratotype Sections and Points (GSSPs) — physical locations in rock outcrops worldwide that mark a precise boundary in the stratigraphic record. There are currently over 70 ratified GSSPs.

Sequence Stratigraphy and Sea Level Change

Modern sequence stratigraphy, developed largely in the context of petroleum exploration in the second half of the twentieth century, interprets sedimentary packages in terms of relative sea level change. As sea level rises and falls in response to glaciation, tectonics, and thermal subsidence, characteristic packages of sediment — called systems tracts — form on continental shelves and slopes. Recognising these packages in subsurface drill cores and seismic reflection profiles has allowed geologists to reconstruct detailed records of past climate and ocean history, as well as to predict the locations of hydrocarbon reservoirs.

Volcanology · Igneous Petrology

Volcanoes and Magma: The Surface Expression of Earth's Interior Heat

Volcanoes are among the most dramatic surface expressions of the planet's internal heat engine — but the diversity of volcanic styles, from gentle Hawaiian effusion to catastrophic Plinian eruption, reflects an equally diverse range of magma compositions.

Volcanoes are windows into the interior of our planet — conduits through which material from depths of 50 kilometres or more is delivered directly to the surface. The more than 1,500 potentially active volcanoes on Earth today are concentrated overwhelmingly along plate boundaries, with a secondary population at hotspots like Hawaii and Yellowstone where mantle plumes punch through the overlying plate from below.

Magma Composition and Eruption Style

Not all magma is alike, and the chemistry of a magma largely determines how its host volcano will behave. The most fundamental variable is silica (SiO₂) content. Low-silica basaltic magmas, which dominate at mid-ocean ridges and hotspot volcanoes, have low viscosity — they flow readily and tend to erupt effusively, producing broad, gently sloping shield volcanoes like Mauna Loa or lava tube systems that carry lava tens of kilometres from the vent. High-silica rhyolitic and andesitic magmas, typical of subduction zone volcanoes, are far more viscous. Gas (mainly water vapour, CO₂, and SO₂) cannot escape easily from thick, sticky magma, and pressure builds until explosive eruption fragments the magma into pyroclastic material — ash, pumice, and volcanic bombs.

"Magma is not simply molten rock — it is a complex mixture of silicate melt, dissolved gases, and suspended crystals, each fraction influencing how the volcano above will behave."

The Products of Volcanism

Effusive volcanism produces lava flows that solidify into basalt (fine-grained, rapid surface cooling) or gabbro if cooled slowly at depth. The surfaces of basaltic flows take two characteristic forms: smooth, ropy pahoehoe forms where lava moves slowly, while rough, jagged a'a forms where lava moves faster and its surface solidifies while the interior continues flowing. Explosive volcanism produces a far more varied product suite: fine volcanic ash that can travel globally in the stratosphere; pumice — a glassy foam of rapidly vesiculated magma light enough to float on water; and ignimbrites, sheets of welded tuff deposited by pyroclastic density currents (fast-moving avalanches of hot gas and rock) that can travel hundreds of kilometres from the eruption site.

The VEI Scale

Volcanic eruptions are ranked on the Volcanic Explosivity Index (VEI), a logarithmic scale from 0 to 8. The 1980 Mount St. Helens eruption rated VEI 5. The Toba supervolcano eruption 74,000 years ago rated VEI 8 — roughly 1,000 times more energetic — and may have caused a global volcanic winter that reduced the human population to as few as 10,000 individuals.

Volcanic Hazards and Monitoring

Modern volcanology employs a sophisticated array of tools to monitor active systems. Seismographs detect the micro-earthquakes generated as magma forces its way through rock fractures toward the surface. Ground deformation measured by GPS and InSAR satellite radar reveals when a volcano is inflating as its magma chamber fills. Gas monitoring detects increasing SO₂ emissions — a reliable precursor to eruptive activity. Combining these signals, volcanologists at well-monitored volcanoes like Kilauea or Etna can often provide days to weeks of warning before an eruption begins, time that has proven critical for civilian evacuations and the protection of infrastructure.
